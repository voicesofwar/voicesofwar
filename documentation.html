Introduction

Overview

The beginning of World War I was marked by a complex web of treaties and alliances between the world's major powers. It started in 1914 with the assassination of Archduke Franz Ferdinand of Austria-Hungary, which led to a series of events that eventually led to the declaration of war. On July 28, 1914, Austria-Hungary declared war on Serbia, setting off a chain reaction of events that quickly escalated into a full-scale global conflict. The war was fought between the Central Powers (led by Germany, Austria-Hungary, and the Ottoman Empire) and the Allied Powers (led by France, the United Kingdom, and Russia).

Our collection contains scans of five postcards, five letters, and the five pages of the diary. These documents were written in 1914-18 in Turkey, Italy, Austria, Ukraine, and Russia. With these letters, the user could see different periods of the war and different places. Letters in Italian are the most numerous in our collection thanks to the work of people who gathered documents from World War I in Europeana. From Turkey, we have 1 letter provided by the Uludağ University History Department and 1 another one from Çanakkale Kızılay Ağadere Museum. Speaking about the manuscripts in the Russian language, we collected three of them – two from Europeana Transcribathon platform, and another from Russian State Public Historical Library.

We will attempt to analyse our letters with the approach defined as Distant Reading, with the aid of the Gis to find and contextualise all the letters in a predefined space, we will apply a sentiment analysis to find the emotions that make up our data and establish a conceptual model.

Historical Background

As mentioned in the introduction, after the assassination of the Archduke Francis Ferdinand on July 28, 1914, Austria-Hungary declared war on Serbia, setting off a chain reaction of events that quickly escalated into a full-scale global conflict. The war was fought between the Central Powers (led by Germany, Austria-Hungary, and the Ottoman Empire) and the Allied Powers (led by France, the United Kingdom, and Russia). Turkey joined the war on the side of the Central Powers in 1914, for expansion, because it was under German influence, for religious ties since the Central Powers included several Muslim states and The Ottoman Empire saw itself as the protector of the Muslim world and also to counter Russia’s expansion. Russia joined the war in 1914 on the side of the Allies because of a strategic alliance since Germany declared war on France which obliged Russia to enter the conflict, for fear of the German expansion, to counter the danger brought by the Austro-Hungarian empire to the Slavic territories and for prestige and territorial expansion. Initially, Italy was part of the Triple Alliance with Germany and Austria-Hungary, but ultimately decided to remain neutral at the outbreak of war. In 1915, Italy switched sides and joined the Allied Powers after secret negotiations promised them territorial gains in exchange for their involvement in the war.

After a brief summary of the beginning of the conflict we will speak in general on the context and the biography of each of our heroes, with the information we were able to gather from the letters, from the metadata we found in Europeana and from our personal research.

Hasan Ethem Efendi and Mehmet Dursun were born in the Ottoman Empire whose decision to join the war was mainly influenced by the Empire’s declining power and status and with its desire to re-establish itself as a major power, in 1914 they signed a secret treaty with Germany which committed the Empire to join the war if Germany went to war with Russia. The conflict was a disaster for the Empire since its army was ill-equipped and ill-prepared for the new type of warfare that was being fought, at the end of the war its territories were reduced, its economy was in ruins and its government was in disarray; this excruciating defeat caused the downfall of the Empire and the rise of modern Turkey.

The Ottoman empire was a Sunni state and it is very evident in the content of our letters, Hasan Ethem Efendi was a martyr teacher and his letter is filled with religious content, there is even a prayer inside his letter which shows the importance that religion played in the empire, as mentioned before the Empire saw itself as the guardians of Islamic faith and culture and used religion to justify their rule and to maintain social order. The empire was also a multi-ethnic and multi religious state and adopted a policy of religious tolerance in order to maintain social stability.

We have a bit more information on Mehmet Dursun who was a Lieutenant at the battle of Çanakkale where he lost his arm due to an enemy artillery shell. The battle is one of the important victories for the Ottoman Empire as it shaped its future years awakening the basis for the Turkish War of independence and the declaration of the Republic of Turkey with Mustafa Kemal Atatürk a commander at Gallipoli and the future founder and president of the Republic.

Janis Farts and Ivan Vasilievich Kucherenko were born in the Russian Empire that joined the war as part of the Allied Powers along with France, Britain and later the USA, the empire had many political and economic interests and also had the military capabilities to endure a conflict. The Russian empire was initially reluctant to join the war in 1914 as it was undergoing a period of political and economic turmoil as the country was devastated by high levels of poverty and inflation which only got worse with the beginning of the war, the government was seen as corrupt and inefficient, and the workers demanded more rights and representations. The morale of the people was really high at the beginning as we can see in the letter of Perekop Ivan Kucherenko a Senior non-commissioned Intelligence officer from Ukraine where the people were enthusiastic about the mobilisation to get the Germans out of their land; most of the people were peasants who were sold the lie of an easy victory. The Russian military faced many setbacks on the battlefield which spread much disillusionment with the leadership of the Army and the Government. Janis Farts was one of the soldiers from Latvia, a teacher before the war, who was wounded in Galicia and taken as a war prisoner by the Austro Hungarians in Austria and was able to return home after the end of the war.

All of these factors contributed to the political turmoil in Russia setting the stage for the Russian Revolution of 1917 and bringing the end of the Tsardom and the beginning of the Soviet Union.

Umberto Lombardo Radice and Michele Villa were born in the Kingdom of Italy, the Kingdom was part of the Triple Alliance with Germany and Austria Hungary but when the war broke out it decided to remain neutral as it was obliged to support its allies only for defensive reasons. The Kingdom, though, was interested in gaining the territories that were considered rightfully Italian: The South Tyrol and Trieste which were under Austrian Rule. It also saw the war as an opportunity to improve its military capabilities and to gain prestige on the world stage. In 1915 Italy signed the Treaty of London switching sides in exchange for territorial concessions of the aforementioned regions. After a brief spike in morale the war slightly shifted towards the Austrian side as the Italian military was plagued by poor logistics and poorly equipped troops. This affected the whole country which underwent a great recession. One of the main reasons for the initial defeats on the Austrian Front was due to Poor leadership as many officers were not prepared to lead and direct the troops, in the High command there was the idea that to have a successful attack you just needed to send more men until the point was captured. Michele Villa was one of the soldiers sent to the Trentino front talks in his letter about how this whole conflict seemed unjust and it sent for no reason men to die, on the Mountains of Trentino is where he will be sent to death. Umberto Lombardo Radice who joined the war at its declaration and fought at the Carsic Border, shows another side of the war, the officer side where the whole conflict seemed like a grand adventure. In the end all of these factors and the inefficiencies of the Italian Military made it difficult for Italy to play a decisive role in the conflict, this changed with a change in the High Ranks of the Italian Army which brought the Victory on the Isonzo Front and the defeat of the Austro-Hungarian Empire.

Research Question and Goals

Questions:

  • How did the war impact the lives of individuals, as reflected in their letters and personal experiences?
  • What narratives were used in different countries that participated in the war to describe the conflict?
  • What sentiments were presented in the documents? How were these sentiments spread among the participants?
  • How did the topic of the war develop over time?

Goals:

  • Digitize and present the manuscripts on a website to preserve and make them accessible to a wider audience.
  • Develop a conceptual model that integrates information from the texts, metadata, and personal research to gain a comprehensive understanding of the authors of the manuscripts and the historical context in which they wrote.
  • Utilise digital tools and techniques, such as distant reading, geographic information systems (GIS) for spatial humanities, sentiment analysis, and topic modelling, to analyse the letters and gain insights into the emotions, themes, and topics expressed by the authors.
  • Provide a representation and interpretation of the results obtained from the digital exploration, shedding light on the individual experiences during the war and their connections to broader historical events.

Methodology

Manuscripts Description and Transcription

First step to digitise our texts was their transcription. It helped us in creating searchable text databases and facilitated the extraction of metadata, which is essential for organising digital scholarly editions. Also, it aims one of the main goals of the digital scholarly editing – exchanging data about cultural heritage. We have transcribed and translated all of the letters into Italian, Russian, and Turkish, so that they are accessible to a wider audience than original texts.

The transcription process involved careful consideration of the historical context and the language conventions of the time. Our aim was to maintain the integrity and authenticity of the original text while ensuring its readability for contemporary readers. We desired to preserve the unique linguistic features, dialects, and stylistic elements present in the original text, because these elements provide crucial insights into the historical period and the cultural context of the document.

We focused primarily on accurately representing the text's words, sentences, and overall structure to provide a clear and accessible rendition of the content. Also, we used original punctuation and formatting to preserve emotional context.
