Introduction

Overview

The beginning of World War I was marked by a complex web of treaties and alliances between the world's major powers. It started in 1914 with the assassination of Archduke Franz Ferdinand of Austria-Hungary, which led to a series of events that eventually led to the declaration of war. On July 28, 1914, Austria-Hungary declared war on Serbia, setting off a chain reaction of events that quickly escalated into a full-scale global conflict. The war was fought between the Central Powers (led by Germany, Austria-Hungary, and the Ottoman Empire) and the Allied Powers (led by France, the United Kingdom, and Russia).

Our collection contains scans of five postcards, five letters, and the five pages of the diary. These documents were written in 1914-18 in Turkey, Italy, Austria, Ukraine, and Russia. With these letters, the user could see different periods of the war and different places. Letters in Italian are the most numerous in our collection thanks to the work of people who gathered documents from World War I in Europeana. From Turkey, we have 1 letter provided by the Uludağ University History Department and 1 another one from Çanakkale Kızılay Ağadere Museum. Speaking about the manuscripts in the Russian language, we collected three of them – two from Europeana Transcribathon platform, and another from Russian State Public Historical Library.

We will attempt to analyse our letters with the approach defined as Distant Reading, with the aid of the Gis to find and contextualise all the letters in a predefined space, we will apply a sentiment analysis to find the emotions that make up our data and establish a conceptual model.


Historical Background

As mentioned in the introduction, after the assassination of the Archduke Francis Ferdinand on July 28, 1914, Austria-Hungary declared war on Serbia, setting off a chain reaction of events that quickly escalated into a full-scale global conflict. The war was fought between the Central Powers (led by Germany, Austria-Hungary, and the Ottoman Empire) and the Allied Powers (led by France, the United Kingdom, and Russia). Turkey joined the war on the side of the Central Powers in 1914, for expansion, because it was under German influence, for religious ties since the Central Powers included several Muslim states and The Ottoman Empire saw itself as the protector of the Muslim world and also to counter Russia’s expansion. Russia joined the war in 1914 on the side of the Allies because of a strategic alliance since Germany declared war on France which obliged Russia to enter the conflict, for fear of the German expansion, to counter the danger brought by the Austro-Hungarian empire to the Slavic territories and for prestige and territorial expansion. Initially, Italy was part of the Triple Alliance with Germany and Austria-Hungary, but ultimately decided to remain neutral at the outbreak of war. In 1915, Italy switched sides and joined the Allied Powers after secret negotiations promised them territorial gains in exchange for their involvement in the war.

After a brief summary of the beginning of the conflict we will speak in general on the context and the biography of each of our heroes, with the information we were able to gather from the letters, from the metadata we found in a Europeana and from our personal research.

Hasan Ethem Efendi and Mehmet Dursun were born in the Ottoman Empire whose decision to join the war was mainly influenced by the Empire’s declining power and status and with its desire to re-establish itself as a major power, in 1914 they signed a secret treaty with Germany which committed the Empire to join the war if Germany went to war with Russia. The conflict was a disaster for the Empire since its army was ill-equipped and ill-prepared for the new type of warfare that was being fought, at the end of the war its territories were reduced, its economy was in ruins and its government was in disarray; this excruciating defeat caused the downfall of the Empire and the rise of modern Turkey.

The Ottoman empire was a Sunni state and it is very evident in the content of our letters, Hasan Ethem Efendi was a martyr teacher and his letter is filled with religious content, there is even a prayer inside his letter which shows the importance that religion played in the empire, as mentioned before the Empire saw itself as the guardians of Islamic faith and culture and used religion to justify their rule and to maintain social order. The empire was also a multi-ethnic and multi religious state and adopted a policy of religious tolerance in order to maintain social stability.

We have a bit more information on Mehmet Dursun who was a Lieutenant at the battle of Çanakkale where he lost his arm due to an enemy artillery shell. The battle is one of the important victories for the Ottoman Empire as it shaped its future years awakening the basis for the Turkish War of independence and the declaration of the Republic of Turkey with Mustafa Kemal Atatürk a commander at Gallipoli and the future founder and president of the Republic.

Janis Farts and Ivan Vasilievich Kucherenko were born in the Russian Empire that joined the war as part of the Allied Powers along with France, Britain and later the USA, the empire had many political and economic interests and also had the military capabilities to endure a conflict. The Russian empire was initially reluctant to join the war in 1914 as it was undergoing a period of political and economic turmoil as the country was devastated by high levels of poverty and inflation which only got worse with the beginning of the war, the government was seen as corrupt and inefficient, and the workers demanded more rights and representations. The morale of the people was really high at the beginning as we can see in the letter of Perekop Ivan Kucherenko a Senior non-commissioned Intelligence officer from Ukraine where the people were enthusiastic about the mobilisation to get the Germans out of their land; most of the people were peasants who were sold the lie of an easy victory. The Russian military faced many setbacks on the battlefield which spread much disillusionment with the leadership of the Army and the Government. Janis Farts was one of the soldiers from Latvia, a teacher before the war, who was wounded in Galicia and taken as a war prisoner by the Austro Hungarians in Austria and was able to return home after the end of the war.

All of these factors contributed to the political turmoil in Russia setting the stage for the Russian Revolution of 1917 and bringing the end of the Tsardom and the beginning of the Soviet Union.

Umberto Lombardo Radice and Michele Villa were born in the Kingdom of Italy, the Kingdom was part of the Triple Alliance with Germany and Austria Hungary but when the war broke out it decided to remain neutral as it was obliged to support its allies only for defensive reasons. The Kingdom, though, was interested in gaining the territories that were considered rightfully Italian: The South Tyrol and Trieste which were under Austrian Rule. It also saw the war as an opportunity to improve its military capabilities and to gain prestige on the world stage. In 1915 Italy signed the Treaty of London switching sides in exchange for territorial concessions of the aforementioned regions. After a brief spike in morale the war slightly shifted towards the Austrian side as the Italian military was plagued by poor logistics and poorly equipped troops. This affected the whole country which underwent a great recession. One of the main reasons for the initial defeats on the Austrian Front was due to Poor leadership as many officers were not prepared to lead and direct the troops, in the High command there was the idea that to have a successful attack you just needed to send more men until the point was captured. Michele Villa was one of the soldiers sent to the Trentino front talks in his letter about how this whole conflict seemed unjust and it sent for no reason men to die, on the Mountains of Trentino is where he will be sent to death. Umberto Lombardo Radice who joined the war at its declaration and fought at the Carsic Border, shows another side of the war, the officer side where the whole conflict seemed like a grand adventure. In the end all of these factors and the inefficiencies of the Italian Military made it difficult for Italy to play a decisive role in the conflict, this changed with a change in the High Ranks of the Italian Army which brought the Victory on the Isonzo Front and the defeat of the Austro-Hungarian Empire.


Research Question and Goals

Questions:

  • How did the war impact the lives of individuals, as reflected in their letters and personal experiences?
  • What narratives were used in different countries that participated in the war to describe the conflict?
  • What sentiments were presented in the documents? How were these sentiments spread among the participants?
  • How did the topic of the war develop over time?

Goals:

  • Digitize and present the manuscripts on a website to preserve and make them accessible to a wider audience.
  • Develop a conceptual model that integrates information from the texts, metadata, and personal research to gain a comprehensive understanding of the authors of the manuscripts and the historical context in which they wrote.
  • Utilise digital tools and techniques, such as distant reading, geographic information systems (GIS) for spatial humanities, sentiment analysis, and topic modelling, to analyse the letters and gain insights into the emotions, themes, and topics expressed by the authors.
  • Provide a representation and interpretation of the results obtained from the digital exploration, shedding light on the individual experiences during the war and their connections to broader historical events.

Methodology

Manuscript Description and Transcription

First step to digitise our texts was their transcription. It helped us in creating searchable text databases and facilitated the extraction of metadata, which is essential for organising digital scholarly editions. Also, it aims one of the main goals of the digital scholarly editing – exchanging data about cultural heritage. We have transcribed and translated all of the letters into Italian, Russian, and Turkish, so that they are accessible to a wider audience than original texts.

The transcription process involved careful consideration of the historical context and the language conventions of the time. Our aim was to maintain the integrity and authenticity of the original text while ensuring its readability for contemporary readers. We desired to preserve the unique linguistic features, dialects, and stylistic elements present in the original text, because these elements provide crucial insights into the historical period and the cultural context of the document.

We focused primarily on accurately representing the text's words, sentences, and overall structure to provide a clear and accessible rendition of the content. Also, we used original punctuation and formatting to preserve emotional context.

1. Manuscripts

Letters in Italian are the most numerous in our collection thanks to the work of people who gathered documents from World War I in Europeana. All documents are marked with postage stamps, and it seems to us that the pages have been written with pencil. Writings on the two cards could be seen quite well, but on the third postcard, it is a bit harder to read since some lines are almost erased.

Three letters from another Italian soldier are written in classical black and blue ink on white paper. To write more accurately, sometimes the author used lining on the paper, which was made with a pencil.

From Turkey, we have two manuscripts written by different people. Both letters are written in black ink on white paper in Arabic letters, and the one manuscript (of Mehmet Dursun contains a postal stamp). One letter provided by the Uludağ University History Department and another one from Çanakkale Kızılay Ağadere Museum .

Speaking about the manuscripts in the Russian language, we collected three of them – two Red Cross postcards also provided by Europeana. The postcards are written in black ink on white paper without lining. Also, we could see the postal stamp, the stamp of the camp, and the sign of the Red Cross which helped prisoners to keep in contact with their families.

From the second Russian character, we have his diary in which he described how he was called into the army and his first days on the frontline. This document was preserved in the Archive of the Russian Academy of Science and was sent to the Presidential Library. The diary has a paper cover and lining and is written in black ink. Also, it contains four additional pages with information from the libraries and archives.

2. Transcription

Our approach was an interpretative representation which involves a deeper level of analysis and contextual interpretation of the transcribed text. We wanted to emphasise not only on reproducing the text but also on extracting and elucidating underlying meanings, cultural nuances, and socio-historical implications embedded within the document. Therefore we did not change anything in the original texts.

2.1 Turkish Texts

During the course of our research, we encountered a significant challenge pertaining to the historical evolution of the Turkish language and its associated script. Preceding 1928, Turkish was predominantly transcribed using the Arabic alphabet, despite the prior implementation of the Turkish Alphabet Revolution during World War I. Notwithstanding the reform, the general populace continued to rely on the Arabic script for written communication. At present, the Turkish language is predominantly transcribed using a modified version of the Latin alphabet, which has been adapted to accommodate the specific phonetic nuances of Turkish. Fortunately, both institutions provided us with transcriptions in the Latin script, obviating the necessity for external assistance in this regard.

2.2 Russian Texts

In contrast, the examination of Russian texts did not pose similar challenges, owing to the clear visibility of the script and the relatively well-preserved nature of the manuscripts. However, a notable historical complexity arose from the usage of the pre-revolutionary Cyrillic alphabet. After the collapse of the monarchy in 1917 in Russia, the Soviet government conducted the reform of the language and grammar to make writing more simple for a wider audience. They wanted to educate people and make reading and writing much more reachable for workers and the peasants also. We found information that after this reform, armed men were sent to the printing houses to remove the cancelled letters specifically to prevent even the technical possibility of printing according to the old standards. In observed texts we dealt with the old grammar rules and letters which do not exist in the contemporary Russian language (І, Ъ, Ѣ, etc.). The share of these words is quite significant – around 17% of the corpus.

2.3 Italian Texts

The Italian texts reveal varying degrees of complexity in their transcription process. Umberto's letters demonstrate impeccable grammar and punctuation, highlighting a refined linguistic proficiency. His adept use of language, particularly in the formal address and detailed descriptions of his military career advancement.

Conversely, Michele's use of language is characterised by basic grammar, frequent errors in accents and double letters, and occasional instances of regional dialect terms. Moreover, the absence of comprehensive punctuation complicates the interpretation of his text. The transcription process for Michele's letters requires a meticulous analysis of numerous linguistic errors, enhancing the comprehension of the content, particularly for non-Italian audiences.


TEI Encoding

The subsequent phase involved the application of XML-TEI encoding to the three textual documents. XML, serving as a metalanguage, establishes a foundational framework for other standards and readily facilitates conversions into diverse formats. TEI, specifically designed for the digital representation of texts, encompasses specifications that accommodate the encoding of various text categories, ranging from screenplays and novels to poetry. The primary objective underlying the implementation of XML-TEI was to enable the comprehensive encoding of the texts, encompassing both logical and semantic dimensions. This encoding strategy utilises tags to explicate the inherent structure of the text and integrate metadata that contributes to a standardised and expeditious interpretation process. Moreover, this methodology facilitates information extraction and enables in-depth analytical investigations and future project development.

For the structuring and organisation of textual data for this project we used TEI version 3.3.0,which we noticed is quite common in other digital scholarly editions, for example Taylor Institution Library. This standardisation ensures compatibility with established protocols and facilitates seamless integration within the broader scholarly community that relies on TEI for text encoding and data exchange.

In the upper line we provided an XML declaration to specify the location of a schema for validating an XML document. It is RelaxNG schema for the TEI standard. The 'href' attribute specifies the location of the schema, and the 'schematypens' attribute specifies the schema namespace.

RelaxNG (REgular LAnguage for XML Next Generation) is a schema language for XML that can be used to define the structure and constraints of XML documents. It is a flexible and powerful tool for ensuring the validity and integrity of XML documents across different domains and applications. We added a link on it to some of the xml files for the future development of the project to check the file structure automatically and process huge amounts of data.

1.TEI Header

This block consists of three core elements <fileDesc> , <profileDesc> and <xenoData>. We encoded not only the content but also the layout of the letters to provide the metadata about them, like it always advice colleagues from the digital scholarly editing. Also, we selected some particular features connected with the domain of our research – comparing the phenomenon of the First World War expressed in letters.

Below we describe the importance of each module of the header.

<fileDesc> contains a full bibliographic description of an electronic file: title and statements of responsibility, details of the publication or distribution, bibliographic notes. Some examples of used tags:

  • <titleStmt> groups information about the title of a work and those responsible for its content.
  • <respStmt> supplies a statement of responsibility for the content of the edition.
  • <publicationStmt> groups information concerning the publication.
  • <availability> supplies information about the availability of a text, in our case - copyright status.
  • <msIdentifier> contains the information required to identify the manuscript or similar object being described.
  • <settlement> contains the name of a settlement such as a city, town, or village.
  • <repository> contains the name of a repository within which manuscripts are stored.
  • <idno> supplies any form of identifier used to identify some object, such as a bibliographic item, a person, a title, an organisation, etc. in a standardised way.
  • <physDesc> contains a full physical description of a manuscript, manuscript part, or other object optionally subdivided using more specialised elements from the model.physDescPart class.
  • <material> contains a word or phrase describing the material of which the object being described is composed.
  • <objectDesc> contains a description of the physical components making up the object which is being described.
  • <supportDesc> groups elements describing the physical support for the written part of a manuscript or other object.
  • <measure> contains a word or phrase referring to some quantity of an object or commodity, usually comprising a number, a unit (in our case – pages).
  • <bibl> contains a loosely-structured bibliographic citation of which the sub-components may be explicitly tagged, in our case – name of the dataset, containing the digitised manuscript.

<profileDesc> provides a detailed description of non-bibliographic aspects of a text, specifically the used languages, the situation in which it was produced, the participants and their setting. Some examples of used tags:

  • <language> characterises a single language or sublanguage used within a text.
  • <person> provides information about an identifiable individual. We also used attributes: sex, age, role etc.
  • <persName> contains a proper noun or proper-noun phrase referring to a person, possibly including one or more of the person's forenames, surnames, etc.
  • <note> contains a note or annotation to add some information.
  • <settingDesc> describes the setting within which a language interaction takes place, or other places otherwise referred to in a text, edition, or metadata.
  • <place> contains data about a geographic location.
  • <location> defines the location of a place as a set of geographical coordinates, in terms of other named geo-political entities, or as an address.
  • <geo> contains any expression of a set of geographic coordinates, representing a point, line, or area on the surface of the earth in some notation.

<xenoData> provides a container element into which metadata in non-TEI formats may be placed. Some examples:

  • <rdf:RDF> encapsulates the entire RDF data.
  • @PREFIX rdfs: specifies the RDF Schema namespace, which is used for defining the vocabulary for RDF data.
  • @PREFIX dbo: refers to the DBpedia ontology namespace, which provides a structured way to represent information from Wikipedia in RDF format.
  • @PREFIX foaf: points to the Friend of a Friend (FOAF) ontology, which is used to define people and their relationships in RDF.
  • <rdf:type> describes the type of a resource in an RDF triple. It specifies the type of a resource by linking it to a class or a resource type defined in an ontology.
  • <rdfs:label> provides a human-readable label or name for a resource. It is used to assign a textual description or title to a resource.

2.Main Texts

Due to the nature of our texts, which comprises historical letters, we employed both standard XML tags for general text representation and specialised tags specifically designed for encoding letters. This approach allowed us to utilise conventional tags for text structure while also incorporating specialised elements that capture the unique features and metadata associated with letters, facilitating a comprehensive and nuanced representation of the epistolary material.

General tags:

  • <facsimile> contains a representation of some written source in the form of a set of images rather than as transcribed or encoded text.
  • <text> contains a single text of any kind.
  • <body> contains the whole body of a single unitary text, excluding any front or back matter.

Special tags:

  • <opener> groups together dateline, byline, salutation, and similar phrases appearing as a preliminary group, especially of a letter.
  • <salute> contains a salutation or greeting.
  • <closer> groups together salutations, datelines, and similar phrases appearing as a final group at the end of a division, especially of a letter.
  • <signed> contains the closing salutation, etc., appended to a foreword, dedicatory epistle, or other division of a text.

3. Translations Encoding

For the encoding of translations, we utilised a similar set of XML tags, with the distinction that facsimile representation was not included, as no facsimile of the translations existed, because the translations were rendered by ourselves. Also, within the tags, we specified the language of the translation alongside other relevant metadata.

The Conceptual Model


RDF Creation

Encoded texts we saved in xml format, and the next step was the making of the RDF. Our goal was to represent concepts and relations expressed in the letters in the machine-readable format with formalised vocabulary of the used ontologies. For this we collected all entities we marked in the encoding and described them in triples as subject – predicate – object. For describing connections we also used our data model.

Final file we saved in the turtle format, which is widely used for expressing data in the Resource Description Framework (RDF) model. Turtle is commonly used to represent and exchange data in the Semantic Web, making it easier to read and understand for both humans and machines because it is human-readable.

We provide some examples of used ontologies, they are mostly domain ontologies like CIDOC-CRM or FaBiO. You can see the others in our RDF file.

  • @prefix fabio: FRBR-aligned Bibliographic Ontology (FaBiO), which is designed for modelling bibliographic information and bibliographic relationships.
  • @prefix schema collaborative initiative between major search engines to provide a shared framework for structured data markup on web pages. This vocabulary helps in marking up web pages and aiding search engines in understanding the content.

In our case mostly we described people and places. Examples you could see here.

<Place/Somalia> rdf:type dbpedia-owl:Country ;
owl:sameAs wiki:Q1045 .

The provided RDF triples describe the resource "Somalia" as an instance of the DBpedia ontology class "Country" using the prefix str "dbpedia-owl". Additionally, the statement specifies that "Somalia" is the same entity as the Wikidata item with the identifier "Q1045" using the prefix "wiki."

<Person/JānisFārts> a foaf:Person ;
being:speaks <Language /Russian> ,
<Language /Latvian> ;
pext:hasMother <Person /Mother_JānisFārts> ;
cwrc:hasUncle <Person /Jacob> ;
pext:hasBrother <Person /Georgiy> ;
pext:hasBrother <Person /Georgiy> ;
ntag:sentTo <Place /Prison> .

The provided RDF triples outline associated with the individual "Jānis Fārts". The first line specifies that "Jānis Fārts" is an instance of the class "foaf:Person." The subsequent lines indicate that this person speaks both the "Russian" and "Latvian" languages, denoted as instances of the class "Language" with identifiers "Russian" and "Latvian." Furthermore, the triples highlight relationships such as "hasMother," "hasUncle," and multiple instances of "hasBrother" for "Jānis Fārts." Finally, the statement "sentTo" denotes that this individual was sent to the place identified as "Prison."

RDF is a crucial technology for the Digital Scholarly Editions. Its significance lies in its ability to represent and describe resources, relationships, and metadata in a structured and machine-readable format. After creating the RDF, we can organise and integrate all our text with other such materials, create rich and interconnected datasets, and foster the creation of comprehensive and interdisciplinary research environments (history and philology for us).


SDE Criteria

Each criterion of the Criteria for Reviewing Scholarly Digital Editions of Patrick Sahle, has been respected.

For the first criterion the bibliography, the title, the editors and the subject of the edition have been provided as well as the contact personal information of the project members.

For the second criterion the texts are presented in a clear and completed format with the addition of images to show the facsimile of the transcribed documents to provide a further sense of completion to our work, allowing the user to view the document in its original format. With future development the project can help in real time users that want to study the conflict with a new way to look at correspondence in real time by using the map.

For the third criterion: the tei model has been used, the documentation has been provided allowing even the user who has no knowledge on the subject to fully comprehend the whole work of the project, the target audience is both the general public and the specialised one.

For the fourth criterion: the interface is easy to use and interpret providing the user with the ability to browse through the letters and to view the facsimile of the document and also to download the xml version of the text; many different perspectives are presented to allow the user to see the different conditions of the common men during this conflict.

Further Developments

Our project can be easily expanded in the future by adding new letters or documents to our roster of documents. The addition of a spatial analysis can enrich our map and add a new way to study the conflict by seeing in real time the position of the people and the places mentioned inside of our documents.
